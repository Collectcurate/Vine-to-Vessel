From Vine

Wine, Ritual, and the Art of the Greek Symposium

Dionysus never really went away.

In Ancient Greece, wine was more than just a drink. It was a gift from the gods, and a force that shaped everyday life. It marked celebration and ritual, structured social gatherings, and accompanied people from childhood to the grave.

To trace the story of ancient wine, we turn to what survives: the vessels. Amphorae carried it across the Mediterranean, kraters mixed it, and cups brought it to drinkers in the symposium. Through these objects, journey of wine through the ancient world comes into view.

As you move thorough the exhibition, follow that journey: from vineyard to vessel, from symposium to festival, and discover how wine connected myth, trade, and human experience.

Act I · Objects 1–3
The Vine & the God
Dionysus and the Origins of Wine

Wine in Ancient Greece was never just a drink.

For the Greeks, it was a gift from Dionysus: a god said to have come from beyond their world, bringing wine with him. But wine did not begin in Greece alone. Its history stretches across older cultures of the eastern Mediterranean, where it was already being made, stored, and consumed.

In these vessels, myth and practice come together. Dionysus appears in the vineyard, while satyrs harvest grapes and tread them into wine. What you see is both story and process: the transformation of grapes into something intoxicating, understood at once as labour and as something divine.

Act II · Objects 4–6
From Vessel to Vessel
The Technology and Trade of Wine

Wine moved.

From vineyard to harbour, from ship to storehouse, it travelled across the Ancient Mediterranean in vessels built for that journey. These containers were not just decorative: they were designed to be carried, stacked, sealed and shipped over long distances.

Each stage of wine required a different vessel. Large transport amphorae carried wine across seas, stamped handles recorded its origin and movement, and mixing vessels prepared it for drinking. Together, the objects in this section traces wine’s passage from commodity to experience.

Act III · Objects 7–10
The Symposium
Drinking, Performance, and the Ritual of the Andron

The symposium – literally "drinking together" – was central to the social life of elite Greek men. Held in a dedicated room in homes called the Andron, it was a structured gathering shaped by ritual, conversation, and performance.

Guests reclined on couches, drank from shared vessels, listened to music, and debated philosophy, politics, and poetry. Wine was always mixed with water, carefully served, and consumed according to custom.

The objects in this section reveal the many roles these vessels played: tools for drinking, markers of status, and carriers of myth.

Act IV · Objects 11–13
Wine, Festivals
& the Dead
The Anthesteria and the World Beyond

Wine marked life at every stage in Ancient Greece. At festivals like the Anthesteria, which celebrated the opening of new wine, children, women, and men all participated in rituals connected to Dionysus.

These celebrations also blurred the boundaries between the living and the dead. During the festival, the spirits of the deceased were believed to return, and wine played a role in both welcoming and sending the departed away.

Vessels for wine were not only used in life but placed in tombs as well. Together, the objects in this last section trace wine’s presence from childhood to the afterlife.

Curatorial Note

From Vine to Vessel: Wine, Ritual, and the Art of the Greek Symposium was developed as part of the University of Toronto MA Art History program in partnership with the Royal Ontario Museum. The exhibition draws on loans from the Penn Museum, the Museum of Fine Arts Boston, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, and the Louvre.


The twelve objects presented here were selected to trace a single, continuous argument: that wine in Ancient Greece was never merely a beverage, but a technology of social cohesion, ritual meaning, and artistic expression — and that the vessels made to hold it were some of the most sophisticated objects the ancient world produced.
