From Vine
to Vessel
Wine, Ritual, and the Art of the Greek Symposium

Dionysus never really went away.

The Ancient Greeks were fascinated with the culture of wine. They celebrated it as a gift from the gods– and for them, it was more than just a drink. Wine was a social technology, a religious instrument, and a marker of identity that followed them from their very first festival to the grave. The wine consumed today is a version of the very drink that the Ancient Greeks made, stored, traded, and poured for one another across the Ancient Mediterranean.

This exhibition traces the journey of wine through the ancient vessels that carried it. From the vineyard to vessel, symposium to festival, and beyond.

Act I · Objects 1–3
The Vine & the God
Dionysus and the Origins of Wine

Wine in Ancient Greece was never just a drink.

For the Ancient Greeks, wine was a gift from Dionysus, a god who arrived from beyond the Greek world. He wasn’t just the god of Symposia or feasting. He was present from the very beginning: in the vineyard, at the harvest, in the moment crushed grapes began their transformation into something intoxicating.

Look closely at the vessels in this section. Think about how myth and labour, stories and reality, twist together like grapevines.

From Vessel to Vessel
The Technology and Trade of Wine

Wine moved.

From vineyard to seller, harbour to ship, storehouse to symposium table, it travelled across the ancient Mediterranean in vessels built for the journey.

Producing, transporting and serving wine required a whole ecosystem of specialised vessels, each shaped for a specific function. These three objects trace that journey, from large transport amphoras to the serving ware of the symposium table.

Act III · Objects 7–10
The Symposium
Drinking, Performance, and the Ritual of the Andron

The symposion, which literally means "drinking together", was the heart of the social life of Ancient Greek aristocrats.

The symposium was special: the Ancient Greeks even had a room designed just for symposia, called the andron.

Men drank from shared cups, listened to music, debated philosophy, and told stories. The objects used in symposia weren't just functional. They were also status symbols, teaching tools, and representations of myth.

The four objects in this section represent the wide spectrum of symposium ware.

Act IV · Objects 11–13
Wine, Festivals
& the Dead
The Anthesteria and the World Beyond

In Athens, wine was not only for the living. The Anthesteria — a three-day festival held in early spring — honoured Dionysus and the opening of the new wine, but it also opened the boundary between the living and the dead.

On the second day, the city's dead were said to roam the streets. Offerings were made. The doors of sanctuaries were sealed. The festival ended with a ritual formula: "Out the door, you Keres. The Anthesteria is over."

The objects in this final section move between the festival and the tomb, tracing wine's role in Greek conceptions of death, memory, and the afterlife.

Curatorial Note

From Vine to Vessel: Wine, Ritual, and the Art of the Greek Symposium was developed as part of the University of Toronto MA Art History program in partnership with the Royal Ontario Museum. The exhibition draws on loans from the Penn Museum, the Museum of Fine Arts Boston, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, and the Louvre.


The twelve objects presented here were selected to trace a single, continuous argument: that wine in Ancient Greece was never merely a beverage, but a technology of social cohesion, ritual meaning, and artistic expression — and that the vessels made to hold it were some of the most sophisticated objects the ancient world produced.
